Days Inventory Outstanding

The "Fridge Problem"

You buy the beer on Monday because it's on sale. The party isn't until Friday.

The Reality

For 4 days, your cash is trapped in glass bottles sitting in your fridge.

You cannot use that money to pay rent. You cannot buy groceries. It is completely locked up in inventory.

"Inventory is just cash wearing a different costume."

02

Days Payable Outstanding

The "Payment Trick"

Before we continue: let's understand how payment timing affects cash flow.

What You Did

Debit Card / Cash

Money leaves your bank account instantly.

Cash is gone immediately.

What Companies Do

Payment Terms (Net 30)

Pay suppliers 30 days later.

Cash stays longer in the business.

The lesson: Delaying payment to suppliers acts like a free loan. Companies negotiate "payment terms" (Net 30, Net 60) to keep cash longer.

In your case, you paid immediately with a debit card — so your DPO = 0 days.

07

The Formula

Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

How long your cash is trapped before coming back to you.

The Formula
CCC = DIO + DSO - DPO

Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding - Days Payable Outstanding

In the House Party Example

4 days (inventory) + 7 days (waiting for payment) - 0 days (paid immediately)

= 11 days without cash

Companies optimize each part to improve cash flow!

08

Cash Flow dynamics differ in industries

Retail Stores

Buy inventory 60 days before selling it. Pay suppliers in 30 days. Wait 7 days for customer payments.

CCC: ~37 days

Tech Startups

Hire developers, build product for months. Revenue comes much later. Burn through cash despite having contracts.

CCC: 120+ days

Amazon

Holds inventory 30+ days but gets customer money instantly. Pays suppliers in 90+ days.

CCC: -60 days!

Profitable companies go bankrupt when they run out of cash, not when they run out of profit.
09

Key Takeaways

1. Profit ≠ Cash

You can be profitable on paper and still go bankrupt.

2. Timing Matters More Than Amounts

When cash moves is often more important than how much.

3. Manage the Cycle

Buy late. Sell fast. Collect faster.
